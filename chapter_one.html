The orb that hung low in the late afternoon sky deceived the mind with its illusion — a crisp ball of ice meant to soothe you through the day. It was that other end of blue: a boiling mass of helium locked in steady fusion into carbon, evaporating the oceans that condense on the night side of the planet and flow across the twilight zones to their repeated doom. No atmosphere thick enough to soften it.

A seam of teal and violet split the heated air — and half a breath behind it, a second seam, green and ethereal, opened to catch the first, the two reaching their full width in the same instant and sealing shut on the same beat. Where they had been stood a pair of porcelain-armored Nekozoku. One taller, with longer, loose white hair; the other with a long ponytail and the same white hair. Feline ears peeked from behind the sculpted cat ears of their Noh masks — the taller’s a smooth white to match her armor, her ponytailed companion’s a feline-cut jade — and below them, the quite-flexible sweep of fur-covered tails.

This was a flat, dry heat with no give in it — and the air this high pulled thin in the chest, enough that even a few chords on this world had left a faint burn behind the breastbone and a dry mineral taste at the back of the tongue. Every edge of their armor caught the gleams of the sharp sunlight differently, the white plate throwing back crisp reflections in its polished coating while the gold trim highlighted a deeper tradition, a reflection of status.

They climbed to the crest of the rise and stopped — alert, surveying the ground below.

Below and ahead, the hangar sat a full kilometer off — small with distance and the heat-shimmer crawling up off the rock between them, its edges seeming to waver and breathe — its open doors wide enough to swallow a city block, the interior darkness breathing cool shadow into the relentless afternoon. The structure was vast in the way that things built for ships are vast. Not impressive. Just large, the way a fact is large. At this range the doors looked small enough to cover with a thumb. They were not, and the mind kept making the correction the eye kept refusing.

Between them and it: a kilometer of flat rocky ground, alien scrub and low broken rock flattened by a burst of wind that was now kicking dust into slow spirals at their feet.

Nothing moved down there.

Which was the problem.

Her hands were at the grips — the twin handles visible above her shoulders, their guards sitting just a few inches above shoulder level, the vertical scabbards riding on their gimbal mounts in the shoulder harness behind her. To anyone watching, reaching for them looked exactly like reaching for her ears. The blades were still in their trans-dimensional scabbards, still on the other side of the threshold, waiting. Beside her Yuki stood with the same stillness, her weight distributed across both feet, Jinsei not yet summoned, the air around her hip carry assembly undisturbed. Only her tail moved — once, a slow arc and back, the one part of her that had never learned to wait.

They watched the ground between them and the hangar.

The breeze kicked across the rocky flat in a low swirl — dust and fragments of dried alien scrub rising in a slow spiral. Where the larger swells turned back on themselves, the charge built and let go: small dry pops of lightning buried in the dust, and now and then a fist-sized bead of it rolling free for a breath before it burst. The wind was the only thing alive out here, and it crackled. Under it, nothing — no engines, no voices, no call of anything living.

Yuki's voice was quiet. Conversational. The way she said things when she already knew the answer and was asking anyway.

"And... you are sure this is the right place?"

Byakko's head moved a fraction of a degree. The slight angle that meant she had looked. The stillness that followed meant she had decided not to say something about it.

A beat. The dust swirled again. Went nowhere.

"Of course it is the right place."

Still not turning her head. Her voice carrying the particular dryness of someone who had never once in her life needed to raise it.

"Would I ever take you to a party at which no one arrived?"

◆

And then the wind died.

Not lulled — died, all at once: the dust dropped straight down, the little lightnings went out, and the silence that came in had a shape. She read it the way she read everything — not a sound but a vibration, the held tension of an intention a breath from becoming an act. Something was about to happen.

Then the roar shook their senses. Two Battledrones launched into the gleaming reflections of that brilliant star low in the afternoon sky. Their overpowered thrust drove a rattling pulse sharply through them. No gliding down or screaming in from out of the sun — they erupted from entrenched positions at maximum velocity, straight up.

And the rocks answered with them. Not the hangar — that sat its full kilometer off, doors open on nothing, exactly where it had been — but the near ground: less than thirty meters out, the scrub and broken stone that had held nothing a breath before resolved into shapes. Angular. Deliberate. The red glowing optical sensors of Lycan cyborg troopers coming online in the low cover, eleven pairs of lights brightening from nothing to full intensity like a row of stars being born in the wrong direction.

The command cyborg had been bred to stand taller, to see the battlefield — and it held the middle, standard formation, but they were spaced too evenly, she thought. Command flanked by jamming cyborgs, sensor arrays sweeping in their unsettling fusion of machine and Lycan. Heavy pounce troopers to either side, taking on the sisters directly. These groups were set across every vulnerable spot — not meant to be seen, but meant to be defeated. There was no hint of tactical purpose to whatever this was, but it was highly inconvenient.

And behind the troopers, low among the stone — the crimson on black of the DarkPack banners hanging loose in the still air.

Not a frontier ambush. Not hired muscle. Not pirates or border raiders taking advantage of a lightly defended world.

It did not matter if Rex knew they were coming — someone close to Rex did. Someone who needed to find what Rex wanted. He would have been here personally if it was that important. But the mystery here was now far bigger than it had been.

The two sisters stood less than thirty meters from all of it and neither of them moved.

Then Byakko spoke. Quietly. The same conversational register she used for everything, in the way that people speak who have decided something and no longer need to perform the deciding.

"Let us greet our hosts, sister. They seem eager to meet our blades."

Her hands moved.

◆

Zero left her left hand in the same instant the rocks came alive — the upward draw converting straight into launch, the blade spiraling away toward the nearest rising Battledrone before the ambush had finished announcing itself.

She did not hurry. A few strides — the hard light flat on the white plate, the distance to the troopers closing under her like a decision already made — before Ichi came up off her right shoulder.

Then the right hand worked.

Down — a vertical stroke from high, splitting the first trooper crown to core before it had finished rising. Right to left — a return cut, clean across the second. Left to right — horizontal and low, taking the legs from under the third, the teal and violet bright at knee height. The rest fell after that without ceremony, until only one was left.

The last she took as she turned — the upward slash carrying her around with it, Ichi lodging clean through the center of skull and neck and upper chest, a canyon opening under it down through the thigh. She came to rest a hundred and eighty degrees from where she had begun, facing away from it, the blade paused in the corpse. Then she flicked Ichi free through the remaining flesh and leveled it in front of her, right hand at her hip — and turned her left hand palm-up to receive Zero, which whirled down into it in a slow spiral, the way a sakura petal comes down looking for the right blade of grass to land on.

She bounced it once in her grip — the habitual little catch she had never once noticed herself doing —

already shaping the reply to the thing she knew Yuki was about to say.

◆

On Yuki's side, Jinsei came up in a single rising arc — the green dissolution boiling the dry afternoon air, the three meters of jade-dark blade humming at a frequency the ground registered before anyone standing on it did.

The four troopers between her and the second drone were in the way.

Then they weren't.

The upstroke continued through the drone without slowing — each half spinning away in its own direction. The blade's momentum carried it through the apex and began its natural arc downward, the dissolution trailing behind it like a flag finding its position. Jinsei settled across her back — not resting on anything, the biological energy holding it elevated, suspended in its own quiet field, the tip barely off the ground, patient and waiting. Her right hand remained on the grip, lightly, the way you keep contact with something that doesn't need you but that you're not quite ready to stop touching.

In the moment her left hand was free, it found the edge of the jade mask.

◆

Yuki peeled the jade mask from her face as the drone halves struck the ground.

◆

Both sisters stood in the settling dust.

Yuki looked at the aftermath. The bent halves. The quiet already returning to the desert floor between them and the hangar doors.

"Was that really supposed to be an ambush?"

Direct. Genuinely curious.

"Or did we catch them at something?"

Byakko turned toward the hangar doors. The combat tension released all at once and the next step forward carried it — knees unbending, weight transferring upward for a single stride, tail lifting, shoulders dropping a fraction. The armor caught the light differently in that step.

To anyone who had spent sixteen ages watching her move, it read as joy.

"The answers are inside, lil' kitten."

The smile was behind the mask. Yuki knew it was there the way she knew everything about her sister — not by seeing it but by the particular quality of the silence that surrounded it.

Byakko reached up and pulled the white Noh mask from her face.

The smile was exactly where Yuki knew it would be.

"After you, Princess."

She did not say anything about it.

She marched into the radiant heat that seemed to have a weight to it.

THE HANGAR

The interior was vast in a way the outside had not warned them about.

The walk to the docking hub was brief, the massive hangar looming there had been a fact — large, industrial, obvious. Crossing out of the relentless light and into the dark felt like traveling by dimension rip, when sound stops being sound and becomes pressure: the heat slid off her shoulders all at once, and the air went still and held — that vacated feel of a room just walked out of. Inside, the hangar was not a fact. It was a condition.

The blue-white starlight came through the entrance that was more like a city block than a structure. The dust catching a glint of something stirred that had not decided to come down — suspended, teasing the long waves of light, going nowhere. The bright shafts threw long shadows off the skeletal docking gantries, stripped of anything a hand could carry, picked clean as a winter branch. Forty meters up the ceiling simply stopped being visible. The floor was dark, once-polished, now scuffed composite, a faint grey-blue.

Four skyships docked in their moorings, hatches open, cargo being transferred, and every one had been stopped partway. Two were mostly loaded — cargo manifests still lit on the terminal screens, containers strapped to their frames, staged for the Hoverlifts to pack them in with the rest. One vessel with its ramps lowered to the floor like the tongues of a hungry beast. The remaining freighter sat far in a corner, stripped of its entire port engine assembly — an ugly mechanical void where the engine had once been seated, anchored firmly enough to bear the punishing thrust of a skyship's drive.

Yuki crossed the threshold first, Jinsei riding its dissolution field at her hip, illuminating the rugged floor with an ephemeral haze, the jade mask loose in her left hand. Three paces in she stopped and turned a slow circle, taking the whole geometry of the place into herself the way she took in everything — not looking at it but absorbing it.

"They were loading," she said. Not a question.

Byakko came in behind her, both blades still drawn, the teal and violet spectrum arcing silent energies, laying a faint dimensional wash across the composite around her boots — color that answered to no lamp in the building. She did not go to the ships. She went to the right-hand wall and walked the perimeter first, reading the edges of a space before its center, the way Arashi had drilled into her before she was tall enough to hold a blade level. The way she had since drilled into Yuki.

"Three manifests are still active," she said, glancing at the terminal screens without going to them. "They were expecting to finish loading and leave here, before us."

"Before us." Yuki gave her familiar echo to her sister's declaration as she followed the trail of crumbs toward the nearest ship — the one with the ramp down — her right hand still resting on Jinsei's grip, the blade hanging patient and elevated a few fingers above the ground, riding on a field of dimensional pressure. The edges of the effect lifted the casual dust in a slow upward curl, the way it does when something moves through a cloud.

"Someone was here recently," she said. "Within the last turn."

"Someone left here recently," Byakko said. Not a correction — a distinction. She had reached the loading terminal and was reading the manifest without touching it. "Here, on these containers. Look at the labeling."

Yuki came to stand beside her. She looked at the manifest. Then turned to look at her sister.

"DarkPack operational prefix on a civilian freight circuit."

"Yes."

"That's way too obvious. It's either very careless or very deliberate."

Byakko did not answer at once. She read the manifest with more than her eyes — there were clues everywhere. This wasn't a commercial manifest, it was a customs checklist: the distributors all listed, but not where the cargo had come from, and the tariff fees not routed to official accounts.

The destination codes were there. Commercial routing, standard freight classes, every field in its proper place. And beneath them, the names. Suppliers. Distributors. Trade houses. A chain of ordinary commercial relationships that read, top to bottom, like nothing at all — a clean manifest for a clean run.

But the tariff fees did not add up — and that, alone, was plain corruption. The corruption by itself would have been enough to alarm her. But this — she knew the names.

Every one of them was Nekozoku. Trading houses, supply brokers, distribution lines that ran through the lawful commerce of the Shogunate's frontier — names her own commissioners brought to dinner, names that surfaced in the quarterly registers without anyone looking twice. And every one had been placed, somewhere along the trade lanes, close enough to a DarkPack hand that the intent seemed deliberate.

So it was made to look like collaboration. Like treason.

She read it through twice. The stillness that came over her was not the stillness of a mind gathering facts. It was the other kind — the stillness of someone who has just felt the weight of the thing in her hands, and understood in the same motion exactly why it had been left where she would be the one to pick it up.

"They stripped their own ship, but not for parts. I do not think these others could even use that engine," Yuki said, from the engine cavity. She had gone down on her heels beside it, studying the floor without laying a finger on anything. Tool marks. Compression scarring, still bright at the edges. Fast work — not careless, but fast, the marks of people working to a clock. She looked up. "The housing was the concealment. The engine came out so the cargo could ride where the engine had been." A pause. "They built this a long time ago. Not improvised — built, for exactly this."

"No," Byakko said. "It was not." A beat. "Yuki — that ship sat there for ages. The engine came out only a few turns ago. So what was there that they needed, now?"

None of this was random.

Not the manifests left conveniently lit. Not the routing codes pitched at precisely the brightness a pursuer could follow. Not the names — Nekozoku merchants chosen with care and set at a careful commercial distance from the DarkPack, near enough to look stained, far enough that proving them clean would take slow, quiet, patient work down a dozen separate trade lines, none of it sayable aloud without putting a crack through every alliance the frontier stood on.

The trap was not the ambush outside.

The ambush outside was the distraction from the trap.

"Yuki," Byakko said.

Yuki stood. Looked at her. She had drifted off as the impact of what this was really about complicated the future.

"We have what Rex intended us to find," Byakko said. Her voice carried the particular flatness it took on when she had arrived somewhere she did not want to be and was being precise about it anyway. "He is not here. The operation is not here. What is here is what he chose to leave us."

Yuki looked at the manifest screens. At the names Byakko had been reading. She understood at once — she had grown up in the same intelligence environment, learned the same things from the same teachers, carried the same instinct for the shape of a planted trail.

"He is pointing us at our own people," she said.

"Yes. He is pointing us at the appearance of our own people," Byakko said. "Which is considerably more useful to him than pointing us at his."

The hangar held its quiet around them. Somewhere off in the dark a drop fell, and then another — a coolant line bleeding, or a seal sweating in the cold — each one landing with the flat patience of a place that has been emptied on purpose and left to keep its own time. Yuki was sure she saw clouds near the ceiling when she arched her head back at an uncomfortable angle to see just how tall this place was.

Yuki looked at the stripped cavity. At the DarkPack banners. At the manifests still running on terminals that had not been shut down because they were not meant to be shut down.

"He won this round," she said.

It was not a question.

"He won this round," Byakko confirmed.

She looked at the manifest one last time and touched her Datakeeper to it, downloading every name into memory, whole, with the thoroughness she saved for things she meant to handle slowly and without sound, in her own time — and never where Rex could stand and watch her flinch.

Then she turned toward the doors.

"We are done here," she said. "We take what he gave us. We take from him what he wanted to lose. We came, we read what he wrote for us, and we leave."

Yuki fell into step beside her.

"And the names?" Yuki said quietly.

"Are a problem for Shiro from now on," Byakko said, "handled so quietly that no one who matters sees us handling it. Which is exactly what Rex calculated we would have to do." A beat. "He is not wrong. He is also not finished. And he will be watching."

They walked back out into the relentless light.

◆

Outside, the heat waited on the alien ground, heavy and unmoving. They spent the entire time searching, blades still in hand. Even relaxed and confident the battle was over, they never let their guard down.

Byakko was ready to leave. She lifted her blades as though she were about to lay them on her shoulders. Ichi came first. As it neared the right scabbard the blade began to give up its definition — not melting, not fading, but going over, the teal-violet bleeding inward from the edges until the steel was something nearer to liquid, and then the scabbard took it, drawing it through the threshold in one clean pull. Zero followed into the left the same way. The handles settled above her shoulders. The scabbards held nothing physical. They never did.

She waited.

Beside her, Yuki brought Jinsei to its carry assembly — the three meters of jade-dark blade softening at the edges as it neared the housing, the green dissolution drawing inward, the steel running liquid for a breath before the assembly pulled it through with the small sound of air closing over a decision. Gone. Recalled. The dissolution dimmed to nothing.

They looked at each other.

Byakko reached up with her right hand and twisted Ichi's grip — a single precise quarter turn. Beside her, Yuki's hand found Jinsei's housing and turned it the same way, a breath behind, the way it always was between them.

The response was immediate and total.

Space tore — twice, a breath apart, the way it had when they came.

Not a door. Not a flash. A rip, visible from every angle at once, as if the air had been seized from behind and pulled inward. For a fraction of a second something that was not light and not darkness reached out of it and took them. Then the alien ground was simply empty, the rip fading to a slow residual impression — an afterimage running backward in the eye of anyone left watching, the absence understood a beat before the motion that made it.

In the corridor outside the east wing of the Tamashii palace, the air split for the same fraction of a second and pushed them out the other side. The teal-violet and the living green drained from the polished stone as the weapons let go of the dimensional path.

Mu was sitting at the end of the corridor.

Waiting.

THE DINNER

The table had been set for twelve, and five came to it first.

Byakko at the head. Yuki at her left. Yamaneko across, in the chair he always took because it let him see the door without seeming to watch it. Shiro down the right. Arashi in the place that was Arashi's whether or not anyone had ever assigned it to her. And Mu, on the low cushion at Byakko's side, where Mu was.

The guests would arrive with the second course. The first belonged to the house.

Byakko had set her question down before the first pour — Kahvan-Da — and then left it where it lay and listened to the room instead, which was its own kind of work and the kind she did best. The kitchen, two walls off. The wine leaving the bottle in a long unhurried note. And underneath all of it the small adjustments a household makes when it has understood that something has changed and has not yet been told what. She let the question wait. They were not a people who needed an answer the same turn they asked it. Time was the one thing the long-lived spent without counting, and they had learned, over a great many ages, what could be lost by spending it badly.

No one briefed her. That was not the shape of this table. A conclusion was not carried across it finished and set down like a plate. It was arrived at, together, out where everyone could watch it come — so that when it arrived, everyone owned the arriving.

Shiro turned the thing over slowly. "The hangar was kept breathing for twelve turns to look like nothing. Cargo in under one name. Out under another. Ordinary as rain." She let ordinary sit, because ordinary had been the entire craft of it. "The booster was lifted out of its cradle by hand. Slowly. By someone unwilling to leave a mark on it." A pause. "And then it was taken off the floor through a Rip-Gate."

No one remarked on the Gate. Space had been torn and stitched at this table's own word more times than anyone present could number; the marvel had gone out of it an age ago, the way the marvel goes out of a door. What held them was not that it had been done. It was the arithmetic of how.

Arashi said it to her plate. She did not need to see the faces to know they had turned to her; she had stopped needing to see anything a long while back. "Three of the thirteen." She turned a slice of something over with the flat of her utensil. "It takes more than one weapon to hold a Gate, and they held one wide enough to swallow a booster, which is no small mouth. So — three. And three of the true ones, spent like that, go dark after. An arc, before they will wake and answer again. Longer." She ate, unhurried. "Which leaves two readings, and I have no liking for either. Either what crossed was worth more to them than three of their thirteen weapons sleeping where they could not be reached — worth that, and the building of a false hangar to carry it — or three is no longer a sum that troubles whoever spent it."

The table held that. It held it long enough that the holding became its own kind of speech.

"The cost may no longer matter to them," Arashi said into it, without lifting her head. "But stealth is their most spent coin."

"They did not use the roads," Yamaneko said quietly. It was not quite agreement. It was the part that had snagged him. "A Fold-Gate would have moved it for the cost of a permit. Instead they tore. You do not tear when you can take the road, unless the road is exactly the thing you cannot afford to be seen on."

"They tore because a Rip leaves no ledger," Shiro said. "Nothing files. Nothing logs. A booster goes onto a hangar floor and simply is not there the next chord, and the only record is in the weapons that did it, and the weapons do not talk." She turned her glass a quarter and did not drink from it. "It is the most expensive way in the galaxy to move a thing quietly. They chose it anyway."

Byakko had gone still.

Not the stillness of attending to them. The further one — the stillness of following a thing that had not yet come into a shape with edges. Her two feline ears found an angle. The human pair followed without being asked.

"We were never the quarry," she said at last, and the words arrived more than they were decided. "To want us dead is to want a difficulty ended. To want us only late buys a handful of chords and nothing else — and no one pays the price of Kahvan-Da for a handful of chords. The whole of it was built to hold us a few chords while that engine came off the floor unseen. We were the noise at the door. The engine was the errand."

"You were expected," Shiro said. "The trap was never built to take you. It was built to make you wait."

"Rex." Byakko gave the name nothing. No heat, no weight of dislike, none of the small theater of it. To know a thing's name was not the same as being moved by it, and she had known this one a long while.

"And what he wants with an engine of that make — that he would spend three weapons and a false hangar to lift it quietly — that, we cannot say." Yamaneko turned his wine and did not drink. "That is the worst of the hangar. Not what it cost him. That we cannot yet name what he bought."

Byakko was quiet a moment. Then she moved her gaze, unhurried, to Shiro. "And he left us a present in the leaving."

"He did." Shiro almost smiled, and did not. "The manifests in the hangar read clean and current when we reached them. A residual, on its face — the system had not finished updating when the place went quiet. Another two chords and there would have been nothing left to read." She let the lie of it hang there for them to see. "A crew that lifts a booster out by hand to spare it a scratch does not forget to wipe its manifests. The manifests were left. And what they carried was a list — false, all of it — naming every Nekozoku merchant doing business within a long reach of that system. Set out for us to find, dressed as carelessness."

"To make us doubt our own," Yamaneko said.

"To make us move against our own. He cannot break the network from outside, so he hands us a reason to break it from within — a roster of our own traders wearing his colors, and lets our care do the rest. Freeze a loyal house, audit an honest combine, and the frontier learns the crown's protection comes with a knife behind it." Shiro's voice did not rise. It never did. "The list is the bait. The trap is what we would do with it."

"Then we will be careful what we do with it," Byakko said. "A false list is only a weapon in the hand that believes it." She turned a fraction toward Arashi. "The engagement itself. Yours."

Arashi set down her utensil. Looked toward Yamaneko, toward Yuki, back to her plate. "Eleven troopers. Two drones. Nothing of ours marked." A beat. "They are getting better."

No one asked who she meant. The not-asking was the answer.

It was Shiro who set the last of it on the table, and tonight it seemed of a piece with the rest — the hangar's own shadow, the same hand reaching further out.

"There is more, and it troubles me past the hangar." She turned her glass and did not drink. "Because if a hand is moving against the frontier, then this is the frontier already moving under it."

"Systems do not go dark anymore. Not in longer than I have held this post — a Fold-Gate is built to last an era, and the roads we keep, we keep. The dead ones are all old: Outer Kesshari three hundred ages gone, the rest of the broken roads older still, dead relays and abandoned outposts the record has long stopped expecting back. Nothing new goes dark." A pause. "So when Leerma went quiet two arcs and a few turns ago, I marked it. A small frontier seat. Nothing on it the galaxy would miss."

She let the next part come slowly, because it was the part that mattered.

"Whoever went looking through Leerma did not stop at Leerma. Leerma pointed onward — and an arc ago, the place it pointed to went dark in its turn." The name she set down by itself, and let it stand. "Oumura. Outer-frontier Usagizoku territory: three institutions, two colonies, and a library of real weight in dimensional scholarship. Its Fold-Gate is dark now, the way Leerma's is dark. But Oumura was not only silenced." She looked at Byakko. "It was emptied. An enormous weight of documents was carried out of that library before the lights went down — carried off, not burned. Someone did not want Oumura gone. Someone wanted what Oumura held, and left the dark behind only to slow whoever came asking."

"It is a search," Yamaneko said. He had set his wine down. "Leerma to Oumura, each one pointing to the next — and he walks between them along the Armistice. Not through Pack ground. Along it. Near enough to use the roads, clear enough that the Pack can swear in full honesty it never carried him. The Armistice is not where he hides." He turned the empty glass. "It is what he searches behind."

"And he has stopped taking places," Shiro said. "He has started taking a name. It comes up the relay chain in the mouths of frightened Usagi who would sooner die than give it — and several have." Her gaze did not leave the Queen's. "Hakase Ren. Of the Oumura Library. He has the library — and he is still hunting the librarian. Which can only mean the documents are not enough on their own: whoever holds them cannot read half of what he took, and needs the one who can." A beat. "You knew him from the Fahng years. You received him yourself. And he is at your table tonight."

Byakko inclined her head a fraction. She had known that already. It was, perhaps, the reason the question had waited.

"So it is all one thing," Yuki said, low. "Kahvan-Da, the dark roads, the scholar who will sit at our table tonight — one hand, one reach, closing on the frontier and on us together."

"So it seems," said Byakko. And she let it rest there. She did not say that something in the seeming had not yet found its place — because she could not yet have said what it was, and a half-felt thing does more harm than good when it is spoken too soon.

◆

The guests arrived with the second course, and the close working air of the first opened out into something warmer, and looser, and far less exact — a long table filling, the wine going round, the first laughter finding its feet. Seen from a step back it was the whole alliance in miniature: a board of peoples who could not share blood and could not do without one another, set side by side in the order old courtesy kept. The Usagizoku, ears tipping toward the good wine before they could think to stop them. The quick small attaché of a Risu trade circuit, already talking. A Seiryū diplomat carrying the still, deep-water weight of his kind. A Lycan envoy of the Armistice, seated in honor — and, by the few who had sat through the first course, watched the more closely for it. And anchoring the whole of it at either end, the Nekozoku: Byakko at the head, Yamaneko at the foot, the house that fed none of these guests and stood over every one of them.

Each was wholly the creature it had been made. Not one of them was a costume.

Across from Shiro sat a face new to the table — Isha Riki, the court's physician, of the Mangusuzoku: a people born to teeth, as the Nekozoku were, and like the Nekozoku never made to run in any pack — self-possessed, solitary, keeping their own counsel and their own law. A rare kinship, in a galaxy that had left the Nekozoku very few. Whatever had been written into her blood was written modestly — demure rounded ears, a short tail full-furred and carried low, nothing that announced itself across a table — and past that, only a warm sharpness to the gaze, and a stillness that let nothing by. She tasted nothing she had not been offered, in the easy manner of a woman whose whole art was knowing what stood in a cup before the cup was raised. Shiro took her measure once, thoroughly, the way she measured anything new at her table — and then, for the while, let it lie.

The household's own steward moved through the service without seeming to move at all: Kit, the MajoDomo, gold even in the amber light. A dozen small things had been set right before the first guest crossed the threshold — a thin place in the cellar quietly eased, one guest's old grievance kept a careful three seats from another's, the courses timed so that none ever waited on a conversation — none of it visible, all of it felt. It was the sort of thing she was simply expected to have managed before anyone thought to ask her to. No one had needed to give her an instruction in a very long while. The word the house used for her — MajoDomo — was an old borrowed thing the Nekozoku tongue had bent until the witch surfaced in it, and the staff said it with affection, and with the faint care one keeps for a person who always knows a little more than she has been told.

And beside the Usagi commissioner, in the seat Kit's quiet courtesy had set for him so that he would not sit among strangers, was a guest Byakko had received herself, which she did not often do.

Hakase Ren, of the Oumura Library. A learned master of the deep stacks — fine-boned, silver, his long ears carried low and still in a way the other Usagi's were not. Byakko had known him from the Fahng years — younger then, both of them, in a galaxy that had been a different shape. He ate little. He watched much, in the manner of one for whom watching had lately stopped being a habit and become a thing he did to remain whole. Where the rest of the table's attention moved with the wine, Ren's did not move at all. He had the stillness of someone holding something he could not set down.

He had not learned tonight that his home was dark. He had carried that in with him, and sat down next to a banker, and been gracious.

The Seiryū diplomat told a small tale of a treaty undone by a single mistranslated courtesy, and was three lines into it before he understood that the Nekozoku were already laughing — the story had outrun him to the table, which made it funnier, which set him laughing at his own telling, which is the best end a diplomat can hope for at any dinner. Byakko laughed. The kind that reaches the eyes. The Risu attaché tried to top it and could not, and was forgiven for trying. The wine went round again.

And beneath the warmth of it she had not, for a single breath, let go of the far end of the table — where the commissioner had drifted, wine-easy, into a quiet reckoning of supply lines with the guest at his elbow. Of all the pleasant noise of the table, that was the thread she kept.

She set down her cup.

"Banking Commissioner Feelon. Of House Ikko. Third Reagent to the Royal Emperor of the Usagizoku." She named him whole, down the length of the table — which in her court was courtesy on its surface and, beneath it, the gentlest possible notice that one had the entire of one's attention. The small radius of conversation around the commissioner contracted into a careful stillness.

She had not needed his supply-line talk to find the name. The name had come to her twice over. Once, dressed as Rex's careless gift. And once — an arc before that, from a wholly separate quarter — as a thing her own people had flagged and could not yet explain. She did not know, sitting there, whether the man was a thread or only a knot the thread had happened to pass through. That was precisely why she asked.

"There was a transfer," she said. "Recent. A considerable one, against repairs to a Fold-Gate. Routed to an engineering concern the Armistice does not permit." She let it rest, mild as the wine. "I would understand the shape of it."

Feelon went the color of a creature that has felt the floor change under it. He had done nothing he could be hanged for and he knew, in the same instant, exactly how it looked — a banker, a barred firm, a great deal of currency, and a season in which roads were going dark. His ears, which had lifted so readily at the wine, lay flat. When he found his voice it was steady only because he made it so.

"The work was real," he said. "The Gate needed it. The concern was — the concern was the one recommended to me." A breath. "By the ambassador."

The word landed in the middle of the table and did not move.

"The Lycan ambassador," Feelon said, because half a truth was worse than the whole of it, and the whole of it was the only thing that might save him. "He recommended the team. He knew the firm. I filed against his recommendation, in good faith, and — and the waiver took its time reaching the comptroller's office for signature, and the permits landed out of their proper order, and I see now how it must read." He had run out of steadiness. "But the name on the recommendation is his."

Every quiet face at the table turned, with great courtesy, toward the Lycan envoy.

The envoy did not flinch. He set down his wine and met the Queen's eyes with the calm of a creature who had expected this, or who was simply very good. "It reads exactly as badly as the commissioner fears, and it is exactly as dull as that," he said. "The firm is the best in the reach at Fold-Gate work — barred for old reasons, political reasons, that have nothing to do with their hands. A government-backed repair, organized in haste and badly, and a waiver that sat in a signature queue an arc too long. By the time the permits caught up to the payment, the order was wrong, and an honest transaction wore the face of a crooked one." He spread one hand, easy. "I recommended them because they would do the work and do it right. I should have shepherded the paperwork. I did not. The fault, such as it is, is a poor one — mine, and tedious."

It hung together. It was complete, and plausible, and entirely the kind of thing that is true.

And it was also exactly what one would say whether it was true or whether one were simply very good at this — and the Queen, who had asked the question for precisely that reason, marked the answer and did not believe it any more than she disbelieved it. She set it where she set such things, unrepeated, unwritten, simply kept.

"Thank you for sharing your knowledge on this matter, Commissioner," she said. "It seems once again our own bureaucracy flares out at us. Infrastructure fails and must be repaired; I see nothing unusual in it, other than the rush to resume commerce meeting the usual bottlenecks." She raised her glass, with a nod to the commissioner and to the envoy alike. "Let us continue our evening, with our friends and our compatriots."

All of them paused, and turned to the Queen, and drank with her.

One does not accuse an ambassador over the soup, and a company that has felt itself useful to the crown guards that feeling long after; the ease she handed back to the room was worth more, tonight, than any answer she might have pressed loose. So she handed it back whole.

And the room took it gratefully. The stillness around the commissioner loosened all at once into the particular pleasure of a table that has helped to clear up a small dark thing for the Royal House. The attaché had always found that firm reliable, he said. The Seiryū diplomat observed that a signature queue was the one true enemy of every government that had ever drawn breath, and got his laugh for it. The commissioner's color returned by degrees. They had been, for a moment, of use — and it is a warm feeling, and they wore it well, and the wine went round once more on the strength of it.

Her own smile reached her eyes, because she willed it to. She was the only one at the table who did not, entirely, set the matter down.

The dinner went on, and ended as good dinners end — slowly, by warm degrees, the courses giving out and the conversation outlasting them, the lamps drawn low, the guests rising in twos and threes to make their courtesies and go. It had been, by every measure the table could take of it, a fine evening. A threat understood. A mystery solved. An alliance fed.

And somewhere in the soft close of it — the lamps low, the warmth gone easy, her people unguarded around her for the first time all evening — the thing that had not found its place all night finally sat down.

She had carried it in as one shape. One hand reaching across the frontier: Kahvan-Da and the dark systems and the hunted scholar, all of a single piece, all of it Rex. But the pieces did not weigh the same in the hand, and it was the weighing that gave it away. The engine had been taken in perfect silence — a false hangar twelve turns in the building, an extraction by hand, a Rip that left no ledger, a gift laid down to send her looking the wrong way down her own table. Someone had spent everything to make certain that errand was never seen at all. And the other — the systems failing in a line, the library carried off whole, the name screamed up the relay chain in the mouths of the dying — none of that could be hidden, and none of it had been, and none of it could have been, by anyone.

You do not take infinite pains to bury one thing and let the other scream across half a frontier, if the two are the same thing. The silence of the one and the noise of the other did not belong to a single purpose.

Two hands. Not one. The engine was an errand, finished and sealed and gone wherever it had gone. The hunt for the scholar was something else entirely — open, loud, unfinished, still moving. And that they were two was far worse than that they were one, because two meant he was doing two things at once, with reach to spare for both, and she had been shown only the edge of either.

She had it, at last, whole. And in the having of it she did not look up — which was the one thing, in all the long evening, that she should have done.

Only Mu, from the low cushion at the Queen's side, did not seem to think the evening was over.

He had been still through all of it, in the way that was only ever Mu. Now he lifted his head, and turned it, and looked — the long, unhurried, unblinking look — toward the far doorway, where the commissioner had drawn the silver scholar a half-step aside, one hand light at his elbow, and was speaking to him low and fast and close, his face too earnest for goodbyes; and where the scholar, who had lost his home and learned tonight that a room full of strangers had begun to do arithmetic about it, was listening, and beginning, slowly, to nod.

Mu watched the two of them move toward the door together.

He did not blink.

No one else was looking that way.

THE BRIEFING

The briefing room at the low end of the turn had the particular air of a place where unpleasant things are made precise.

Shiro was already there — the far end of the long table, white robes luminous in the cool even light, the frontier open on a data surface before her and resolving and reresolving as the systems caught up to themselves. Two points on it burned brighter than the rest. Two systems gone dark.

Byakko took the chair at the head and did not sit so much as arrive in it. "What do we have that the table did not."

"Two silences and a lie." Shiro turned the map a quarter without touching it. "Leerma went quiet the better part of an arc ago — not the three turns the dispatch log claims. Someone reached into the archive and moved the date, quickly, more worried about the record than about being caught having touched it. And Oumura went dark behind it, along the same line." She let it settle. "No road runs to either now. Whatever was done there, we learn it by going — or we do not learn it at all."

"Then we go to both. We cannot afford the one we leave." Byakko looked at the two burning points. "Not a strike. A question, asked in two places at once — what was done here, and what was taken. We send the ones who can read the answer off the walls."

The door opened.

They crossed it the way they always crossed a threshold: three bodies, one motion, no one leading and no one led — the way a hand does not lead its own fingers. Three glazed cat-masks caught the light and held it, each its own color, each bound in navy cloth wound several times around — the yellow mask wrapped across the eyes, the blue across the ears, the red across the mouth. A maxim the old empire had carved a hundred ways, worn here instead of spoken, and turned inside out: behind each binding was not a closed sense but an open one, the dimensional energy poured into the place a sense should have been and filling it past the brim.

Twin-tailed, all three — auburn, blonde, jet-black — the mutation the galaxy makes perhaps once in a long age. Three of one age was a thing no record accounted for, and Byakko had long since learned to leave the not-accounting alone, the way one leaves a debt one cannot yet pay.

Arashi was already in the room, the chair nearest the wall, Kaze at her hip, the chipped cup nowhere in sight — which meant she had come to work. Her blind face turned a few degrees as they passed, the way a root turns toward water it cannot see, and turned back. She had carried all three off a burning world when they were small enough to carry at once, and in all the ages since had never called them anything but hers — and never once aloud.

"They are ready," she said, to no one. Not quite a report. Not quite a blessing.

"I know what they are ready for," Byakko said. "I am deciding what to ask of it."

◆

It was Mine who spoke — Mine always spoke, the only one of the three with a voice that went outward, and the still point the other two's scattered speech bent toward and cohered into words. The yellow mask turned to the map she could not see, and she read it anyway.

"Four," she said. "Not two."

Shiro's head came up a fraction.

"Two are dark — Leerma, Oumura, you have those. Two more are only thin, further along the same line, where the signal still answers and answers a little wrong." She did not point; she had no need to look to know where a thing sat in the substrate. "Whoever walks this line did not begin at Leerma, and has not ended at Oumura. Leerma is only where the hand grew careless enough to be caught."

"A search," Yamaneko said. He had come in during the entrance, in the way he had, so that no one quite saw him arrive. "We named it that at the table. She is giving us its length."

"And its hand."

Beside Mine, the blue mask had gone still in the particular way that meant Kika was no longer entirely in the room. Wisps rose off her tails — more than the idle drift she always carried, a brightening — and every head at the table marked it the way one marks a lamp turned up. That was the cost the three had always paid: they could hide the words, never the fact of them. Only Mine heard what was inside. She tilted her head, gathering it.

"One operator," Mine said. "Kika has the edited records — all of them, the whole line — and they are one hand. Not the same orders. The same hand. Three credentials inside our own archive, one person behind the three." A pause, listening past the first of it. "She has been hearing him a long while. She says he is tired."

"Tired," said Byakko.

The wisps came again, folding back on themselves, and Mine's head followed them. "A careful hand stays careful. A tired one repeats itself — and he is repeating himself. He has not found the thing he was sent for, and he is beginning to fear what is done to those who do not find it." The wisps turned a color the room had no word for, and Mine went quiet a moment, and the quiet had effort in it. "There is more, and I cannot carry it. Only — that she is not entirely unkind about him." She let it go, unfinished, the way she had long ago learned to let Kika go. "It does not always come. I am sorry."

No one filled the silence. They had all watched Mine fail to carry her sister before. It was not a thing you helped with.

Then the red mask's weapon answered — Yōgantō, on its rack two rooms off, glowing where it lay, a banked red waking in the air near Iwa's hand — and Mine put the words to it without dressing them.

"Iwa says a frightened hand is the most dangerous kind. It stops being careful before it stops being able." A beat. "She says reach him before the fear does."

It was not gloom. It was a verdict, and it was right, and Iwa was the one willing to be the one who said it.

"Then we will not give him the time to rest." Byakko turned to the map. "The pepper, Shiro. Plainly, in order."

"It was never the prize." Shiro drew one thread forward — Leerma, and the small farming combine that had been its only reason to exist. "The Blue Pepper of Leerma carries a resonance in its stabilization chemistry — a signature older than the colony, a thing it grew without ever knowing it grew. When the system thinned, the signature changed, and that change is what first told us a frontier no one watches was being read for something dimensional. The pepper is the breadcrumb. It is how we found the trail at all. It is not what walks it." She let the thread fall back. "He is following a scent the pepper happened to carry — to Oumura, where the thing it belongs to sat on shelves, in a language he cannot read without the one who shelved it."

"Hakase Ren," Yuki said — flat, certain, from the chair at Byakko's left, where she had been listening with the stillness she kept for the parts she had no piece in yet.

"Hakase Ren," Byakko agreed. "Who sat at my table last evening, whole and gracious, and whom I did not press." Something in her voice did not move at all, which was how those who knew her heard that it had cost her.

◆

Byakko stood, and the room reorganized around her standing.

"Two questions, two dark systems, the same locked door at each. No road runs in, and no ship will carry you. Only the Rip will — and the Rip answers only to a weapon. No ordinary agent reaches a dark system, no fleet, no envoy; only the few who can tear their own way through, one body at a time, and step out the far side whole. The going costs nothing." She let it sit. "That is not where the price is."

It was Arashi who named the price, because she had counted it before anyone else thought to look. "How few of us there are to send. That is what he spends when he darkens a road — not your strength, your number. A dark system can be reached by perhaps a dozen beings in this whole galaxy, and the better part of that dozen is sitting in this room, and he is cutting it into pieces." Her blind head tilted. "He cannot beat the few of you in any one place. So he is seeing to it that the few of you are never all in one place again."

It was a worse arithmetic than any weapon's, and every weapon in the room knew it.

Byakko set the plan down the way she set all things down — plainly, once. "Oumura is written in a language only the three of you can read. The library carried off, the road torn down, the manner of the tearing — all of it dimensional, all of it on the walls. Yama takes you. Read it, bring it back, and do not stay to be read in return." She turned. "Yuki, Shiro, and I take Leerma. The older silence. If the trail has a root, the root is there."

"And the engine," Yamaneko said. "Kahvan-Da."

"Waits." The word had edges. "It is the thing we know least and can spare least for, and I will not send half a team chasing a mystery while two systems sit dark and a hand walks our frontier. Say it plainly, so we all carry it: we are doing two things at once because he has left us no choice, and a third thing we are letting fall — and that is the cost of being two hands short against an enemy who is not."

Mu, from the cushion by the head of the table, lifted his head. He had done nothing else all turn. He did it now: turned toward the door, the long unhurried look, a beat before the sound came.

The door opened. It was Kit.

The MajoDomo did not come to war rooms. The household ran on the principle that it need not know what was decided here, only that it had been — and Kit had never, in memory, crossed this threshold. She crossed it now, gold and composed and entirely out of place, and the wrongness of her being there arrived a full breath ahead of her words.

"Forgive the intrusion." She inclined her head — to Byakko, and only to Byakko. "Two of last evening's guests did not sleep in the rooms prepared for them. Commissioner Feelon called for a private craft a chord after the table broke, citing urgent affairs at his House. He did not leave alone." A pause, precisely weighted — the pause of one who has already measured the size of what she carries and means to hand it over level, so that it does not spill. "Hakase Ren went with him. In haste. They are beyond the system by now."

The silence after had a different grain than the silences before.

Byakko did not move for a moment. Then she did the thing she had not done at the close of the dinner, the thing she had failed to do at the one moment it would have served: she looked up.

"There it is," she said, quietly, to no one. "Two hands. I held it last night like a coat folded over the arm — and never put it on." She turned from the map. "It was never one thing. The engine is one errand, finished and gone. Ren is another, and it did not end when he reached my table; it only paused, while he was safe. The moment he stopped being safe it began again — and I am the one who frightened him out of the only door that was shut against it."

"You did not frighten him," Yuki said. "Feelon did."

"Feelon ran because I moved the floor under an innocent man. Ren went with him because beside a frightened fool was the safest place left to him." Her voice did not rise; it never did. "It does not matter whose hand. He is out, and moving, and the thing hunting him does not need to catch him — only to find him. One person, one rip, and he is gone, to us or to Rex, and whoever finds him first ends it in a breath." She looked at the two dark points that had been, a moment ago, the whole of the problem. "The plan is wrong. It was built for the threat I understood. I have just been shown the one I missed."

She rebuilt it in a breath, aloud, the way she rebuilt everything — so that the arriving belonged to all of them.

"Oumura still must be read, and only the three of you can read it. You go — alone. I would not send you into a colony's grave without an anchor if I had one to spare, and I no longer do." Along the wall, three masks took it without flinching.

Then the red glow woke again, and Mine voiced it, plain. "Iwa says we were always going in alone. The anchor was a kindness, and a short turn spends its kindnesses first." She did not soften it, because softening it would have put a gentler sister's words in Iwa's mouth. It was not gloom. It was true. And Iwa was the one willing to say it. Arashi's blind face did not turn, and the not-turning was the loudest thing in the room.

"Yama — not Oumura. You go after Ren, you and Yuki both, the two heaviest hands I have, because keeping him from Rex is a matter of reaching him first, and reaching him is done with speed and force and nothing gentle. Up the relay chain, after the same dying voices Rex is following. Get to the end of them before he does."

"He has a head start," Yamaneko said. An accounting, not an objection.

"Then close it." She turned last to Shiro. "You and I keep Leerma — the cold root. It should be the two who can read what a careful, tired, frightened hand leaves behind when it believes no one will ever come to look." A breath. "Three teams. Three questions. The engine waits, and waits longer now, and I have made my peace with none of it."

She lifted her mask from the table. "Then go."

And they went — not toward any door. There was none in this for them and never had been; the only road a dark system leaves open is the one a weapon tears, and they had come prepared, because preparedness was simply what they were. They did not file out. They left from where they stood.

The three opened theirs together. Mine's bow and Iwa's blade called the seam up out of the air — and Kika needed no blade for it, only a turn of the hand the world declined to refuse — and a line that had not been there came apart on a thread of dimensional green. Yellow, blue, red; six tails in their slow separate times; and then the seam, and then the place where they had stood, empty. No procession. No threshold walked to. Here — and gone to Oumura.

Around the room the others tore their own ways to their own dark fronts, until the war council had not so much ended as come apart into its several absences.

Arashi did not rise to see them off. There was nowhere left to see them to. She only turned her blind face to the closing seam where her three had been — the way she had once carried them across one just like it, off a burning world, when they were small enough to carry all at once — and held there a moment after it sealed.

Mu watched it all and did not blink. At the close of the dinner Byakko had looked inward, alone, a turn too late. This time, in the breath before they tore the world and were gone, every one of them had been looking the same way Mu was — out, at the same dark.

THE OTHER SIDE

Three turns' travel from Tamashii, in a stretch of the frontier the official charts called economically inactive and the DarkPack called convenient, there was a facility that appeared in no registry.

The main chamber was large, and built to feel larger — screens across two walls running operational feeds, signal maps, the constant information-metabolism of a covert war in full forward motion. The amber-and-red light the DarkPack favored gave everything the quality of perpetual crisis, which was the point. Crisis kept the blood up. Crisis was a thing you could lead.

Speaker Vox owned the center of it.

One meter tall, the human-proportioned head and hands set on a frame that genetics had cheated of its vertical due — a fact he had never once noticed, because the conviction filled whatever the bones did not. He stood at the podium with the red cybernetic eye burning, a chamber full of DarkPack staff turned toward him like plants toward a poisoned sun, and he was not briefing them. He was tuning them.

"They will tell you," Vox began — low, the way he always began low — "that we are a frontier nuisance. A grievance. A pack of mongrels scratching at the edge of the civilized galaxy." A pause. "They have told you that your whole lives. They told your mothers that. They will tell your pups that — if we leave them the breath to do it."

A sound moved through the room. Lower than words.

"They sit in their nine bright capitals and call themselves the order of things. The Nekozoku on their thrones. The Usagi in their libraries. The prey — grown so fat and so certain they have forgotten they were ever prey at all." His voice began to climb. "They have forgotten what the dark is for."

The sound came up a step. Some of them were on their feet.

"We are going to remind them. Not raid them. Not bleed them at the edges and call it a war — I am done with the edges." The finger rose, the familiar one, the one the whole room waited for. "Every road. Every capital. Every shelf of their precious library, every throne of their precious house — all of it, all at once, until there is no quiet corner left in this galaxy where a prey thing can whisper itself the old lie that it is safe." He let it hang. "Rex does not want a border moved. Rex wants the map erased."

He had them. You could feel the instant he had them — the chamber tipping, the breath held.

"They put a collar on the rest of our blood," he said then, softer, almost tender. "Our own cousins. They signed the treaties. They sat at the prey's table and learned to beg so prettily they forgot they had ever been wolves at all." The red eye swept the room. "They call that peace. We have a truer word for it." A growl moved under the silence. "The leash."

He let it burn.

"So I will ask you only once." Vox spread his arms; the red eye blazed. "Are you ready to let loose the True Pack?"

The chamber did not answer in words. The DarkPack never did, when it mattered. It answered the way the blood in them answered — every throat in the room opening at once into a single rising howl that climbed the walls and the screens and would not stop, a sound with no language in it and no need of any. Vox stood in the middle of it with his head back and his arms open and drank it like water.

◆

At the edge of the light, where the howl thinned, the command did not howl.

Beta Kijo watched the room come apart at the seams with the fond, proprietary half-smile of someone watching her pups learn to bite — the golden fire at the tip of her blade the brightest thing at that end of the chamber. Thinker Xort stood beside her, arms folded, red cybernetic eye on Vox, wearing the look she kept for things she found useful and disgusting in equal measure. Kage, the Lycan, stood a careful half-step apart from both, the way she always stood — present, attentive, committed to nothing her face could be read for.

"He is insufferable," Xort said, beneath the howl.

"He is effective," Kijo said.

"As I keep telling you. Those are not exclusive."

It was about then that the sound from their immediate left became impossible to ignore.

The Doctor had not looked up for any of it. He sat where he always sat, a little apart, hunched over the steel tray he carried everywhere, and he was eating. What he was eating was not a thing the others chose to name. It had been alive recently enough that the question of how recently was the part that turned the stomach — and he worked through it with the slow, absorbed, joyless precision of a man performing surgery on his own dinner: each separation deliberate, each portion considered, a wet and methodical sound that somehow carried further than the howl. He paused. Selected something. Held it to the light between two fingers, examined it with professional interest, and put it where it went.

Xort's red eye twitched. "Must you."

"He is feeding," the Doctor said — mildly, and not of himself; he never spoke of himself — of something on the tray that had, apparently, still been deciding whether to. He resumed.

Kage, who had stood unflinching through three campaigns and a thing in the south she did not discuss, turned her face away and breathed through her mouth. "I have seen field hospitals," she said, to no one, "I would sooner have eaten in."

"He keeps the staff healthy," Kijo observed.

"He keeps the staff afraid of being unhealthy," Xort said. "There is a difference, and he is it."

The Doctor, serenely, ate.

◆

Kijo had, by then, acquired an admirer.

He was young, and DarkPack to the bone, and the howl had filled him with the particular courage that gets young soldiers exactly as far as it has ever gotten any of them. He had drifted to the edge of the command's circle somewhere in Vox's crescendo and spent the back half of the speech working himself up to her, and now — flushed with the war-cry, certain of his own rising fortunes — he made his approach.

Kijo let him.

That was the thing the ones who knew her watched for, and the young ones never did: Kijo let you. She turned the half-smile on him and let it warm a degree. She let him say the bold thing, and then the bolder thing, and she laughed low at the second one in a way that made him stand straighter. She let him take the half-step that closed the distance between admiring the Beta and presuming upon her. She let him get all the way there. She let him be sure.

"You have a quick mouth," she told him, fondly.

"I have other quick things."

"I believe you," Kijo said — and the golden fire was through him before the smile had finished, one clean unhurried motion that opened him hip to hip and gave the floor everything he had been so proud of. In front of the entire command. In front of the nearest third of the howling room.

Near them, the howl faltered.

He looked down at himself with an expression of enormous surprise. They always did. Kijo watched him discover it with the same fond attention she had given the flirting — as if the two halves of the evening were one continuous courtesy she was paying him — and when his knees went she stepped back the exact distance that kept the gold of her blade clean, and let him fold onto the floor he had just become part of.

"Anyone else," she said pleasantly, to the staff gone rigid around her, "have other quick things?"

No one did. The howl resumed, elsewhere, louder — eager to be anywhere the Beta was not looking.

The Doctor reached over without rising, drew his steel tray a prudent few inches clear of the spreading edge of the young man, and went on eating.

◆

From the back of the chamber, a door opened.

The light changed — not the way light changes when something grand arrives, but the opposite. It diminished. The amber-red pulled back toward the corners as though something at the center were drinking it, and what stayed took on a hue with no clean name: a dying green, a corrupted red, bleeding across the screens and the floor and the faces and the cooling heap that had lately been an admirer. Wherever Rex stood became the center of the room — not because he claimed it, but because everything else quietly resigned the post.

Alpha Rex crossed the chamber. The staff did not turn to look at him. They had learned not to.

He passed the cage without slowing — one hand closing on the chain at the Usagi prisoner's throat and lifting, walking on in the same motion so the captive's feet found the floor only in scrambles — and dragged him up onto the dais. The throne behind it was compacted skulls fired into a morbid porcelain, a thing that would have been absurd on anyone else and was simply Rex on Rex. He took Regret into both hands.

The weapon answered. The corruption along it deepened; the acid-green and dark-red of the thing inside pulsed once, slow, with something that might have been appetite.

"I need to be closer," Rex said, in the voice that came from the depth it always came from. He looked at the screens, not the prisoner. "Tell me where it is."

The Usagi, dangling, had completed some private accounting and arrived at a sum he could die with.

"The next relay station. The one you passed leaving the Oumura library." A pause. "Where is Hakase Ren."

The Usagi looked directly into the dark-green absence where the left of Rex's skull had been — the place Regret had built in the space Hope once took, not a wound and not a repair but something grown, patient and permanent and alive the way only very old things are alive.

"Eat glass, Mutt."

He spat into the absence. The faint vapor of it was the only reaction Rex gave.

Regret swung. The Usagi's defiance ended the way defiance ended in that room.

Rex stood in the diminished light with the weapon in his hands, and the whisper arrived — not entering, arriving, from the inside, from the place his mind used to be:

Leerma leads to Grenriyan. Grenriyan leads to Tamashii. Your vengeance is waiting for you there.

He recoiled from it. Then he did not. The revulsion and the wanting came together, indistinguishable from each other, indistinguishable from himself, and he sat down on the throne of skulls and let the wrong light hold him.

Across the chamber Vox had climbed back onto his rhetoric like a man remounting a horse mid-gallop, and the howl was rising again to meet him, and over it and under it and through it he found the room's eye one last time and gave them the only word the night still needed.

"Closer," he said.

And the whole dark room howled it back — and the road they were howling down ran, though none of them yet knew it but Rex, and Rex did not yet know that he knew, straight to the white palace on Tamashii. To a queen who had, that very turn, sent the last of her few scattering to the far corners of the galaxy, and kept not one of them home.
